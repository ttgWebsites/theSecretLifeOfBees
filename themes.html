Themes

  • A Strong Feminine Community
  • Early on in the novel Lily struggles to find femininity due to the lack of strong female relationships she has
  • Her mother died when she was at a young age and the only mother figure in her life is Rosaleen who cares for her and raises, however Rosaleen is arrested and again she must search for a motherly figure to help teach her femininity
  • After she is separated from Rosaleen, Lily does find another source of femininity, and that is through the Boatwright house
  • Lily finds a group of women at the Boatwright house that are all caring and act as mother figures for Lily, they make her feel welcome and she can finally learn her role as a woman
  • The Power of Stories and Literature
  • Lily's early life is filled with hardships and the one escape she finds is through literature
  • She is a very good reader and loves books as they open a new world that allows her to seek comfort
  • Lily uses the literature to keep herself smart as well as strong in her day to day life
  • Lily receives a notebook from Zach in which she can keep journals and write her own stories
  • Through writing, reading, and story telling Lily is able to see hope for her own future, and in turn make something of herself
  • The Irrationality of Racism and Stereotypes
  • The author helps the reader understand throughout the novel that racism is not only wrong, but also founded on untrue stereotypes as well
  • Growing up Lily has been raised with slight prejudices against African Americans that she must overcome throughout the story
  • Lily is raised by Rosaleen for a while after her mother dies
  • Rosaleen is an uneducated laborer who is a housekeeper
  • Lily initially has a prejudice towards African Americans and thinks them all to be coarse and uneducated people
  • However, after she meets August Boatwright, who is quite the opposite, she begins to realize that blacks are just like whites, and that prejudices should be thrown out the window, because no matter race, humans are all equally deserving of the same treatment
  • Later on in the novel when Lily meets Zach, an African American man, she also has to put aside her prejudices as she begins to fall for him
  • Growing up she was taught to never love black people, and that black men are far less attractive then white men
  • She and Zach fall in love and it is due in large part to Lily overcoming her racism and irrational prejudices
